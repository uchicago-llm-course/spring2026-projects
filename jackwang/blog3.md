Redoing most of the experiments:

My old experiment is entirely wrong. The issue is that I would train my AI detection model on domain A(e.g writing prompts) with ground truth(human responses) + responses generated by models A*, and then evalute it on dataset A(still writing prompts) with different sets of ground truths(different human responses) responses generated by models B*(different set of models). The issue is that even though the training and test data is different, the fact that it is from the same domain leads to an unfiar advantage, as our model could just be simply exploiting domain specific patterns in dataset A.

Reformulating new experiments:

Basically switch dataset A with dataset B when doing evaluation. For example, if my invariant model is trained on writing prompts, then it should be evaluated on wikipedia articles. I rewrote the training, evaluation, and pangram comparison pipeline so that it is in line with the correct experiment.

Evaluation compared to Pangram:

First, we evaluated against pangram's EditLens RoBERTa on their own dataset in their provided open source repo.

| | AUROC | Acc | F1 |
|--|--|--|--|
| EditLens RoBERTa | 1.0000 | 1.000 | 1.000 |
| Ours | 0.7300 | 0.640 | 0.667 |

The pangram accuracy is extremely high because EditLens was trained on GPT-4.1 outputs — the same model used to generate the AI samples in their own dataset. This is essentially a home-field advantage. Our model gets 0.73 with no exposure to GPT-4.1 at all during training.

Next, we ran both models on our Wikipedia held-out split — a domain neither model saw during training, with generators (Gemma-7B, Phi-3-mini, DeepSeek-7B) that our model also never trained on.

| | AUROC | Acc | F1 | FPR | FNR |
|--|--|--|--|--|--|
| EditLens RoBERTa | 0.7009 | 0.538 | 0.538 | 0.596 | 0.192 |
| Ours | 0.8725 | 0.795 | 0.750 | 0.269 | 0.077 |

Under fair conditions our model outperforms EditLens by +17 AUROC points. EditLens flags most human text as AI (FPR=0.596), while our model is much more precise with very few missed AI samples (FNR=0.077).

Adverserial Paraphrase attack on Pangram:

Take Pangram's own dataset, and then have deepseek paraphrase 100 of the AI generated responses. Then, evaluate Pangram's model on these transformed responses and see how much worse it gets.

I wrote the experiment pipeline for this, and ran it.

| | AUROC |
|--|--|
| Original AI (GPT-4.1) | 1.0000 |
| Paraphrased AI (DeepSeek-7B) | 0.9992 |

Interestingly enough, the attack failed. Pangram is definitely learning more than just GPT style. It could be overfitting to the dataset itself, but I need more experiments in this region.
