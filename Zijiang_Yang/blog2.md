This week, I focused on revising the training objective in my TextCLIP project. My dataset contains one prompt paired with four responses generated by different LLMs, so I wanted the loss function to better reflect that structure. Instead of training on independent `(prompt, response)` pairs, I tried a multi-response version that jointly trains on `(prompt, response1, response2, response3, response4)` and treats all four responses as positives for the same prompt.

The main insight from this week is that making the loss look more faithful to the dataset does not necessarily lead to better representations. My original intuition was that, since one prompt can have multiple valid responses, it would be better to pull the prompt embedding toward all of them at once. In practice, though, this change seems to weaken the contrastive signal. The four responses for the same prompt are often quite different in wording, specificity, and even quality, so treating them as equally positive may blur the representation instead of sharpening it.

## Modify training loss

Originally, I used a standard symmetric CLIP-style contrastive loss on single prompt-response pairs. For each batch, the model learns to match each prompt with its paired response while pushing it away from other responses in the batch, and vice versa.

This week, I changed the loss so that each prompt is matched against all four of its associated responses simultaneously. On the prompt-to-response side, the prompt distributes its probability mass over four positives instead of one. On the response-to-prompt side, each of the four responses maps back to the same prompt. Conceptually, this seemed like a more natural formulation for my data.

## Training behavior: revised loss vs. original loss

The revised loss did learn something, but the overall behavior was not as strong as I hoped. Under the new objective, the training loss steadily decreased, and the validation recall@1 improved from roughly 0.18 to around 0.43. Validation loss also dropped substantially in the early stage of training. So the model was clearly not failing outright.

However, the validation curves flattened fairly early. After the initial improvement, both `val_loss` and `val_loss_norm` stopped getting better and started to oscillate slightly. The validation accuracy against random rewrites also moved up and down in a fairly narrow band, mostly around 0.70--0.78, without showing the same consistent upward trend as before. This suggests that the model could fit the training objective, but the learned representation did not generalize as well as expected.

By contrast, the original pairwise loss behaved much more cleanly. Its average loss decreased smoothly throughout training, and the evaluation accuracies were consistently stronger. The accuracy against random rewrites rose to around 0.86--0.88, the accuracy against random chosen responses reached around 0.82--0.84, and the accuracy against corresponding responses was around 0.77--0.78. The average accuracy ended near 0.81. Overall, the original loss was both more stable and more effective.

## Post-training analysis

I also did some analysis of the trained embedding space.

In the chosen-vs.-reject similarity plot, the model achieved an accuracy of about 0.768. That means in roughly 77\% of examples, the chosen response had higher similarity to the prompt than the reject response. This is encouraging, because it shows that the model has learned a meaningful compatibility signal. At the same time, the separation is not especially strong. Many points lie close to the diagonal, and there are still many cases where the reject response gets a similarity score comparable to the chosen response. So the model captures topical relevance to some extent, but the margin is still limited.

I also did visualization on prompt, rejected response and chosen responses embedding vectors by using TSNE and UMAP. Prompt, chosen, and reject embeddings are not fully mixed together, which means the representation space is learning some useful structure. In several local regions, prompts appear closer to chosen responses than to rejects. But the overlap is still substantial, especially between chosen and reject responses. This suggests that the model is learning a partial alignment signal, but not yet a completely clean separation between stronger and weaker responses.

## Challenges and roadblocks

The biggest challenge this week was realizing that a more complicated objective is not automatically a better one. The multi-response loss made sense conceptually, but it exposed a real issue in my data: multiple responses to the same prompt are not interchangeable positives. Some are better than others, and some may even introduce noise for representation learning.

Another roadblock is that my current evaluation is still somewhat narrow. Right now, I mainly measure whether the model can distinguish chosen responses from rejected or random responses. That is useful, but it does not fully capture whether the learned representation aligns with broader notions of helpfulness, harmlessness, or human preference.

## Next steps

Next week, I plan to expand the evaluation setup and move beyond purely on-topic matching. In particular, I want to design additional evaluation tasks around toxicity and human preference, and then fine-tune the model specifically for those tasks. My goal is to test whether the learned representation can capture more practically important alignment dimensions, rather than only semantic similarity between prompts and responses.
